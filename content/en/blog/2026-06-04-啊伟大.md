It’s been a long time since I last visited Hokkaido.

The last time I came was to go skiing in Niseko, Sapporo. Although I was still quite young, I clearly remember the heavy snowfall that fell day and night without stopping. Every day when I opened the door, I seemed to see snowdrifts taller than a person, with nothing but a vast expanse of white before my eyes. That was the impression Hokkaido left on me back then: the “caves” shoveled out of the snow at the entrances of shops, the black granules scattered across the sidewalks, and the snowdrifts of unknown depth everywhere—so deep that even when I stuck my whole arm in, I couldn’t reach the bottom.

This trip to Hokkaido, however, came about by a twist of fate. I consider myself very lucky to have made so many friends in such a short time since arriving in Japan. I had just started school in Tokyo, and during my first winter break, I casually mentioned to them that I might be planning a trip to Hokkaido. Someone enthusiastically asked if I wanted to join them, and before long, I was on a plane headed for Hokkaido.

![]()

The long-missed cold air filled my lungs, like a baptism that cleansed the confusion of the past few months. We then wandered through the streets, climbed over small mounds of snow, and looked down on the city from the hotel’s high-rise: the snow-capped mountains in the distance, the roads in the middle, and the layered buildings up close—all blanketed in pristine white snow.
