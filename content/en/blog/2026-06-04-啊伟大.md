The moment the ferry glided into the harbor, the sea was as calm as a mirror. A red lighthouse on the left and a white lighthouse on the right stood symmetrically at either end of the bay, with the boundless gray-blue sea and sky stretching out between them. That was my first glimpse of Hokkaido—so quiet it felt almost unreal.

![](/uploads/67a856bde95654810e98178bf19ffbfc.jpg)

![](/uploads/b2e1d892c3f41d511bb2514705ec3040.jpg)

After disembarking from the ferry, I boarded a train to continue northward. Peering through the crack in the window, I saw the tracks stretching straight into the distance, with snowdrifts on either side pressing everything down low. In this black-and-white world, only the tracks were dark, like two lines etched into the snowy plain. The train car was heated, and the windows were fogged up; the window frames framed the scenery outside like a painting.

![](/uploads/a00ee409c97c493fc974cc028e6779d1.jpg)

On the streets of Otaru, the snow lies thick. A rickshaw driver, hunched over, is straining to push his rickshaw across the icy pavement—it’s empty, with no passengers, but he pushes with the same earnest determination. Behind him stand old stone buildings, their arched doorways sharply framing the light. There is a stubborn resilience in this scene, unique to winter.

A taxi parked by the roadside at night is covered with a thin layer of snow, its “Yamazaki” sign glowing with a cool light in the darkness. Water droplets clung to the windows, and the driver’s silhouette was faintly visible. Pedestrians on the street walked quickly, their backgrounds blurring into a few indistinct figures. The night in Hokkaido is neither bustling nor lonely—it is simply cold.

![](/uploads/ddf285527eb1073a14607b56ce312f2d.jpg)

During the day, I visited a snowfield on the outskirts. There is an art museum there, welcoming every visitor with a wide, arched entrance. Standing inside the arch and looking out, the backlighting turned everyone into silhouettes—some walking, some pausing. The concrete pillars and the snow-covered forest in the distance all looked both modern and desolate.

![](/uploads/110f88a000492d5b04ebfb4eda01b8f9.jpg)

Out on the snowy plain, someone fell backward into the snow, landing on their back with all four limbs in the air, half their body buried. The black-and-white image makes the scene look a bit absurd, yet strangely amusing. The snow in Hokkaido is thick and soft enough to make such a dive worthwhile.

![](/uploads/0b2f83989868d6f6f7bf7c98978001db.jpg)

Before leaving that snowy plain, I spotted several small stone statues of Jizo by the roadside, half-buried in the snow, standing neatly beside a stone stele inscribed with “Namu Amida Butsu.” The twilight had already begun to paint the horizon red, and they stood there quietly, their expressions serene. I wonder how many winters they’ve stood here.

In the evening, I drove along a mountain road. The utility poles and power lines on either side of the road divided the sky into irregular geometric shapes, while the outlines of the mountains blurred in the last vestiges of twilight. This road seemed to have no end—and perhaps it didn’t need one.

Earlier in the day, I passed through a farming area where a massive metal warehouse stood solitary in the snow. The wind and snow had etched mottled patterns across its side, making it look like an abstract painting. The sky was a uniform gray-white, devoid of any extraneous elements.

![](/uploads/0ca6fe941a2142e151772c06b9e58e84.jpg)

The final stop was the famous Moai statue complex. At dusk, they stood in a row against the twilight, arranged from smallest to largest, their profiles all facing the same direction. They had no expression—nor did they need one. A hint of orange still lingered on the distant horizon, and the silhouette of a deer appeared on the snowy slope, silently gazing back at the statues.

![](/uploads/b2e1d892c3f41d511bb2514705ec3040.jpg)

Perhaps it is at moments like this that you suddenly realize: the most unforgettable part of a journey is never the sight itself, but rather a twilight hour when the light is just about to fade—and that indescribable, quiet sense of fulfillment.
