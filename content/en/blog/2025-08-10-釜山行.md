Busan is a small city nestled on a hillside. Buildings crowd together, climbing all the way from the coast up to the mountainside. Power lines in the alleys slice through the sky, while seagulls look down from above at this city that looks as if it’s been squeezed by a giant hand, then follow the tide back to the rocks.

![](/uploads/dscf0909.jpeg)

I’ve been to Busan a few times.

Before the pandemic, there was a certain vitality here: the smoke from the restaurants on Haeundae’s shopping street, the bustling voices of the crowd, the scent of the sea mingling with the aroma of food, and the shouts of the owner of the skewered meat stall next to me... It was noisy and unrefined, yet it felt comfortable—I never grew tired of it.

But coming back this time, I feel like something is missing.

![](/uploads/dscf0951.jpeg)

What exactly is it? It’s the shops that used to line the street, their windows plastered with faded “For Sale” signs—some have been there for ages, the edges curling up, yet no one bothers to take them down. Shops where I once had pleasant chats with the owners are now gone, and the once-bustling streets have become deserted. Perhaps it’s just the off-season.

Busan has never been Korea’s most dazzling city. But its port, its fish market, and its hillsides have always been there. It’s rugged, and it is precisely this ruggedness that defines Busan, making it a city with a unique character.

![](/uploads/dscf0920.jpeg)

I walked from Haeundae to Mipo Station and took a capsule train to Cheongsapo. Although the journey was only a little over two kilometers, it felt like it took a long time.

![](/uploads/dscf1157.jpeg)

As soon as you arrive at Cheongsapo, you can see the two lighthouses beneath the tracks. People often sit on the embankment fishing, and occasionally children play nearby. This tranquility is mesmerizing; you don’t need to say much to be drawn into that mood and savor the moment.

![](/uploads/dscf1178.jpeg)

At four in the morning the next day, I walked out onto the beach and found the scenery completely different from the day before. The sea hadn’t yet awakened; the sky was just beginning to lighten, holding back a sliver of sunrise—a density so tangible you could almost touch it.

![](/uploads/dscf1285.jpeg)

Past noon, Haeundae Beach

Busan is an interesting place.
